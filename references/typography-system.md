# Typography System

This is the heart of the skill. A quote card lives or dies on its typography. The job is to
build hierarchy, make the key point land faster, and make the card feel worth saving. Read
this before locking the prompt.

## Hierarchy

Every card has three layers. The eye must hit them in order.

- Dominant element: the hero — the quote, the takeaway, the big number, the term. There is
  exactly one. It is the largest and the reason the card exists.
- Supporting layer: context, a definition, the list points, a highlighted line. Quieter
  weight, smaller size.
- Meta layer: source, page number, author, attribution, tiny "来源". The smallest thing on
  the card, calm and out of the way.

Do: make the hero unmistakably first, the support clearly second, the meta a quiet footnote.
Don't: give two elements equal weight, or let the source compete with the hero.

## Type Scale & Weight

Strong contrast is the whole game. The hero is much larger and bolder than the rest — not
slightly bigger.

- Hero line: large and heavy. It should read first from across a room.
- Support: a clear step down in size and weight. Readable but secondary.
- Meta: small and light.
- Aim for a real ratio — the hero roughly 2–3× the support, the meta smaller again.

Do: make scale jumps obvious; let the hero dominate.
Don't: set everything at one size — that flat, even look is the PPT trap, and it kills the
hierarchy.

## Alignment

Pick ONE alignment and hold it across the whole card.

- Left-align for lists, steps, definitions, and anything with multiple lines — it gives a
  clean reading edge.
- Centered only for short quotes and single hero lines — it stops looking calm once there
  are more than two or three lines.
- Never mix: a centered title over left-aligned points reads as two competing cards.

Do: commit to one edge.
Don't: center some lines and left-align others on the same card.

## Spacing & Margins

Negative space is a feature, not waste. The card breathes.

- Keep generous, even outer margins on all four sides. The hero is never crammed to the edge.
- Use consistent spacing between lines and between layers; let the layers separate by space,
  not by boxes or rules.
- A short quote can sit in a large calm field of empty space — that emptiness is what makes
  it feel collectable.

Do: leave room; let the idea sit in space.
Don't: fill every corner, or pack text edge to edge to use the whole frame.

## Palette

Restraint reads as quality.

- Base: paper / off-white / a soft tint. Ink: a deep, calm dark for text.
- One accent color at most — on the number, the key word, a small block, or a rule.
- Optional subtle paper grain, or a single flat accent block behind the highlighted line.
- Max two fonts: one for the hero (a clean strong sans or a refined serif), one for support.

Do: paper + ink + one accent; two fonts max.
Don't: rainbow palettes, gradient glow on text, six colors, a different font per line.

## Decoration Restraint

Decoration is allowed only when it is quiet and serves the hierarchy.

- Allowed: a thin rule to separate layers, a large faint quotation mark, a small accent block
  behind one line, a numeral as a list marker.
- Forbidden: clip art, stickers, emoji piles, mascots, gradient glow, sparkle bursts, drop
  shadows on drop shadows, a title bar on everything.

Do: at most one quiet graphic accent.
Don't: let any decoration outweigh the words, or add ornament just to fill space.

## The PPT Test

Before generating, ask: would this look like a slide?

- If every line is the same size → fix the scale; enlarge the hero, shrink the rest.
- If there is a colored title bar across the top → remove it; let the title lead by weight.
- If points are equal-weight bullets → set a clear title and quiet numbered points.
- If the frame is full corner to corner → cut content or add margin until it breathes.

A card passes when the eye lands on one hero first, the layers are clearly sized apart, the
alignment is consistent, and the whole thing has room to breathe.
